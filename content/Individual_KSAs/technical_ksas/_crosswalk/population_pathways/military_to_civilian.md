## How to Use This Document

This document is a coaching tool, not a client handout. Use it to orient the intake conversation with a transitioning service member: identify the client's primary MOS category, navigate to that profile, and use the Civilian Sector Destinations table as the anchor for a discussion about which sectors align with their existing competency base. The adjacency scores reflect empirical overlap between the military MOS domain's cluster_tags and each civilian sector's KSA library — a MEDIUM score means the sector has 4–6 shared competency domains with the MOS profile; HIGH means 7 or more. Do not present these scores to clients directly; translate them into plain-language statements about transferability during the session. Where a client's MOS spans two profiles, review both and synthesize.

### Profile 1: Cyber & Information Operations
**Representative MOSs / AFSCs / Ratings:** 17A/C/E (Cyber Operations Officer/Warrant Officer), 25B (IT Specialist), 25N (Nodal Network Systems Operator), 25U (Signal Support Systems Specialist), 35Q (Cryptologic Network Warfare Specialist), 35T (Military Intelligence Systems Maintainer)

**Fort Gordon relevance:** This is CaliberPath's highest-density MOS population. Fort Gordon hosts U.S. Army Cyber Command (ARCYBER), the Army Cyber Center of Excellence, the Georgia Cyber Center (civilian extension), and a large contractor ecosystem supporting DoD cyber operations. Many 17- and 25-series soldiers reach the 10–14 year mark and elect transition rather than continuing to the 20-year mark; others separate at the 4–6 year point with strong technical foundations but limited civilian credential recognition. The GS civilian cyber workforce at Fort Gordon (GS-2210 series) represents a parallel B2B population.

**Civilian Sector Destinations — from Adjacency Matrix:**

| Sector | Adjacency Score | Bridging Domains | Coaching Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Core IT & Cloud | MEDIUM (score 2) | Cybersecurity, Cloud Architecture, DevOps, Network Engineering | Strongest technical match; CMMC compliance background is a direct market differentiator for Georgia Cyber Center corridor employers |
| AI, Data & Quantum | MEDIUM (score 2) | AI, Analytics, Data Engineering, Machine Learning | Growing demand in Fort Gordon contractor ecosystem; data science credentials (e.g., CompTIA Data+, Google Data Analytics) bridge the gap |
| Government & Public Administration | MEDIUM (score 2) | Digital Services, Policy & Compliance, Risk Management, Program Management | GS civilian pathway (2210 series) is the most common short-term destination; DoD RMF experience translates directly |
| Telecommunications | LOW (score 1) | Network Engineering, Systems Integration | Narrower match but accessible; fiber and wireless infrastructure buildout in CSRA creates demand for network-experienced hires |
| Finance & Insurance | LOW (score 1) | Cybersecurity, Fintech, Compliance | Emerging demand for cyber-aware compliance officers and fintech security specialists; longer credential runway required |

**Top Entry KSA Domains:** Cybersecurity & Information Security · Cloud Architecture · Network Engineering · Data Analytics · Risk Management & Compliance

**Typical Entry Roles:**
- Information Security Analyst (O*NET 15-1212.00) — most direct translation; high demand in CSRA contractor market
- Cloud Engineer / Cloud Administrator — strong pathway for 25-series with AWS/Azure exposure
- Network Administrator — accessible for 25B/25N with CompTIA Network+ or CCNA
- IT Project Manager / Program Analyst — for 17-series officers and senior NCOs with systems integration leadership
- Cybersecurity Compliance Specialist (CMMC, RMF) — niche but high-value in the DoD contractor corridor

**CaliberPath Service Match:**
- *Career Compass Coaching:* Open with a credential gap analysis against civilian cyber job postings in the CSRA market. Many clients have deep operational experience but lack the civilian certification language (CISSP, Security+, AWS SAA) that employers screen for. The first two sessions should map existing competencies to NICE Framework work roles and identify the minimum credential investment for market entry.
- *Stream 4 Assessment Focus:* UPLS Digital Literacy, Systems Thinking, and Problem Solving competencies are primary. Leadership competencies at Advanced level for 17-series officers.

### Profile 2: Intelligence Analysis
**Representative MOSs / AFSCs / Ratings:** 35F (Intelligence Analyst), 35G (Geospatial Intelligence Imagery Analyst), 35L (Counterintelligence Agent), 35M (Human Intelligence Collector), 35P (Cryptologic Linguist), 35N (Signals Intelligence Analyst)

**Fort Gordon relevance:** The 704th Military Intelligence Brigade, INSCOM subordinate units, and ARCYBER intelligence elements are all Fort Gordon residents. 35-series soldiers represent a significant segment of the transitioning population — particularly 35F analysts who have worked in all-source analytical environments. Many develop strong data-manipulation, structured analytical technique, and briefing skills that translate well to civilian analytical roles, but often lack recognized civilian credentials or degree qualifications that ATS systems screen for.

**Civilian Sector Destinations — from Adjacency Matrix:**

| Sector | Adjacency Score | Bridging Domains | Coaching Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| AI, Data & Quantum | MEDIUM (score 2) | Analytics, Data Science, AI Applications, Intelligence Analysis | Most direct domain match; civilian "analyst" roles in data and business intelligence align well with all-source analytical tradecraft |
| Government & Public Administration | MEDIUM (score 2) | Policy Analysis, Risk Management, Research Methods, Compliance | GS 0132 (Intelligence) and 0343 (Management Analyst) series are common federal landing points; state and local government also accessible |
| Professional & Scientific Services | LOW (score 1) | Research Methods, Analytics, Report Writing, Risk Assessment | Consulting firms (especially defense-adjacent) value structured analytical thinking; proposal writing and research roles accessible |
| Finance & Insurance | LOW (score 1) | Risk Management, Compliance, Data Analysis, Regulatory Affairs | Risk analyst and compliance officer roles leverage intelligence analytical frameworks; longer ramp time in financial domain knowledge |

**Top Entry KSA Domains:** Data Analytics · Risk Management · Research Methods · Intelligence Analysis & Reporting · Geospatial Analysis (35G-specific)

**Typical Entry Roles:**
- Data Analyst / Business Intelligence Analyst (O*NET 15-2051.00) — structured analytical skills transfer directly; Python or SQL credentials bridge the gap
- Risk Analyst / Compliance Analyst — leverages threat assessment and vulnerability analysis tradecraft
- Intelligence Analyst (contractor) — most direct transition; requires active clearance maintenance; major employers in CSRA include SAIC, Leidos, Booz Allen
- Research Analyst / Policy Analyst — accessible for senior 35Fs with officer or WO equivalents; requires strong writing portfolio
- Program Analyst (GS-0343) — federal pathway for those preferring to remain in government service

**CaliberPath Service Match:**
- *Career Compass Coaching:* Address the credential-versus-experience gap early. Many 35-series clients have significant analytical depth but struggle to translate classified experience into unclassified resume language. Sessions 1–3 should focus on competency articulation — building the civilian vocabulary for what they actually do. If a client has or is pursuing a clearance-required contractor role, the pathway is shorter; if targeting civilian private sector, a data credential investment (6–12 months) is typically recommended.
- *Stream 4 Assessment Focus:* UPLS Critical Thinking, Research & Analysis, and Written Communication competencies. For 35L/M: interpersonal and cross-cultural competencies.

### Profile 3: Signal & Communications
**Representative MOSs / AFSCs / Ratings:** 25C (Radio Operator-Maintainer), 25E (Electromagnetic Spectrum Manager), 25L (Cable Systems Installer-Maintainer), 25S (Satellite Communications Systems Operator-Maintainer), 25U (Signal Support Systems Specialist), 11-series with comms collateral duties

**Fort Gordon relevance:** Fort Gordon is the historic home of the U.S. Army Signal Corps and remains the Signal Center of Excellence. 25-series soldiers are trained here and frequently complete multiple assignments before separating, building deep hands-on infrastructure and maintenance experience. The CSRA's growing data center corridor (Cyber District) and expanding telecommunications infrastructure create local demand that aligns well with this population's practical skills.

**Civilian Sector Destinations — from Adjacency Matrix:**

| Sector | Adjacency Score | Bridging Domains | Coaching Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Telecommunications | LOW (score 1) | Network Engineering, Systems Integration, Infrastructure Maintenance | Most direct sector match; 25S (satellite) and 25C (radio) experience maps to field technician and systems specialist roles |
| Core IT & Cloud | LOW (score 1) | Network Engineering, Systems Administration | Requires additional IT credential investment (CompTIA A+/Network+) but the foundational infrastructure knowledge is strong |
| Manufacturing | LOW (score 1) | Maintenance & Repair, Systems Integration, Electronics | Unexpected but viable; industrial electronics and maintenance roles leverage the hands-on technical background |
| Construction & Infrastructure | MARGINAL | Infrastructure, Maintenance | Entry-level accessible for clients open to trades-adjacent technical work; conduit installation and low-voltage cabling are natural on-ramps |

**Top Entry KSA Domains:** Network Engineering · Telecommunications Infrastructure · Systems Integration · Maintenance & Repair · Electronics & Radio Frequency

**Typical Entry Roles:**
- Telecommunications Technician (O*NET 49-2022.00) — direct translation; field service roles with carriers or tower companies
- Network Technician / Field Engineer — accessible with CompTIA Network+ and hands-on router/switch experience
- Systems Administrator (entry-level) — for 25U with desktop and server administration experience
- Field Service Engineer — industrial electronics; defense contractors and manufacturing employers in CSRA
- Low-Voltage Systems Installer — accessible without significant credential investment; strong local demand

**CaliberPath Service Match:**
- *Career Compass Coaching:* This population often undervalues practical technical skills because they lack white-collar credential recognition. The coaching priority is building confidence in the civilian market value of hands-on infrastructure expertise, then identifying the minimum credential investment (typically CompTIA Network+ or a vendor certification) that unlocks the next salary tier. CSRA-specific employer mapping (data centers, defense contractors, telecommunications providers) should anchor sessions 2–3.
- *Stream 4 Assessment Focus:* UPLS Technical Proficiency and Problem Solving competencies. For senior 25-series NCOs: Leadership and Coaching competencies at Intermediate level.

### Profile 4: Aviation & Logistics
**Representative MOSs / AFSCs / Ratings:** 15-series (Aviation — 15B/D/F/P/T/U/W), 88-series (Motor Transport — 88H/M/N), 92-series (Quartermaster & Supply — 92A/F/G/Y), 68-series (Medical Logistics — 68J)

**Fort Gordon relevance:** Fort Gordon's Combat Sustainment Support Battalions (CSSBs), aviation maintenance detachments, and the large footprint of logistics units supporting ARCYBER and other garrison functions generate a steady logistics and aviation mechanic transition population. 92-series supply specialists are one of the most common MOS groups at any installation and represent a high-volume coaching population. 88-series drivers and dispatchers are a secondary segment.

**Civilian Sector Destinations — from Adjacency Matrix:**

| Sector | Adjacency Score | Bridging Domains | Coaching Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Transportation & Logistics | HIGH (score 3) | Logistics, Supply Chain, Automation, Maintenance, Planning, Safety, Quality | The repository's strongest sector pair — 7 shared domains with manufacturing; this population's skills are the most directly transferable in the entire MOS catalog |
| Manufacturing | HIGH (score 3) | Automation, Maintenance, Quality, Safety, Supply Chain, Planning, Logistics | Via the transportation–manufacturing HIGH pair; 15-series mechanics and 92-series supply specialists both find strong footholds |
| Construction & Infrastructure | MEDIUM (score 2) | Maintenance, Logistics, Safety, Compliance, Supply Chain | Particularly relevant for 15-series airframe mechanics; construction equipment operation and heavy maintenance are adjacent |
| Mining & Extraction | MEDIUM (score 2) | Maintenance, Safety, Logistics, Planning, Automation | Regional opportunity (kaolin mining, Middle Georgia); also pipeline operations in CSRA corridor |
| Agriculture & Natural Resources | MEDIUM (score 2) | Supply Chain, Safety, Compliance, Logistics, Sustainability | Agribusiness logistics and fleet management for large agricultural operations |

**Top Entry KSA Domains:** Logistics & Supply Chain · Maintenance & Repair · Safety Management · Automation & Operations · Quality Control · Planning & Scheduling

**Typical Entry Roles:**
- Logistics Coordinator / Supply Chain Analyst (O*NET 13-1081.01) — direct translation for 92-series; strong local demand from manufacturing and distribution employers
- Warehouse / Distribution Center Supervisor — accessible for 92-series NCOs; Club Car (Textron), Kimberly-Clark, and John Deere are CSRA employers in this space
- Aviation Maintenance Technician (FAA A&P) — for 15-series with eligibility; FAA A&P certification is the credential bridge; Augusta Regional Airport and helicopter operators are local employers
- Fleet Manager / Dispatcher — for 88-series; local government, school districts, and logistics companies hire from this background
- Operations Supervisor / Production Supervisor — manufacturing floor leadership for senior NCOs with production and quality experience

**CaliberPath Service Match:**
- *Career Compass Coaching:* This population has the clearest civilian pathway of any Fort Gordon MOS group — the adjacency data supports it. The coaching focus is salary calibration (military logistics experience is often undervalued at civilian entry), credential identification (CDL for 88-series, FAA A&P for 15-series, APICS CSCP for 92-series), and CSRA employer targeting. Sessions 1–2 should confirm the target role, sessions 3–5 should build the resume and credential plan.
- *Stream 4 Assessment Focus:* UPLS Operations Management, Safety & Risk Management, and Project Planning competencies. For 15-series: Technical Proficiency at Advanced level.

### Profile 5: Leadership & Management
**Representative MOSs / AFSCs / Ratings:** All NCOs (E-5 through E-9) and Officers (O-1 through O-6) in any branch with primary leadership/management duties; particularly relevant for soldiers in final 2–4 years of a 20-year career or WOs/senior NCOs electing early transition

**Fort Gordon relevance:** Fort Gordon's garrison command, ARCYBER headquarters, the Signal Center, and the many subordinate units all generate senior NCO and officer transitions. This population tends to have more time to plan the transition and more financial runway to invest in credentials, but often faces the hardest identity transition — from positional authority in a hierarchical system to influence-based leadership in a civilian organization. GS civilian leadership positions (GS-13 through GS-15) are a natural near-term target for O-4/O-5 officers.

**Civilian Sector Destinations — from Adjacency Matrix:**

| Sector | Adjacency Score | Bridging Domains | Coaching Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Government & Public Administration | MEDIUM (score 2) | Leadership & Management, Policy & Compliance, Risk Management, Program Management | Federal GS pathway (0340/0343/0301 series) and state/local government leadership roles are the most common first civilian role for senior officers |
| Professional & Scientific Services | LOW (score 1) | Project Management, Research Methods, Consulting, Leadership | Management consulting (particularly defense-adjacent firms) actively recruits O-4 and above; MBA pairing accelerates this pathway |
| Administrative Support Services | LOW (score 1) | Operations Management, Project Coordination, Compliance, Human Resources | Operations director and COO-adjacent roles in mid-size organizations; nonprofit sector also accessible |
| Nonprofit & NGOs | LOW (score 1) | Leadership, Community Development, Program Management, Volunteer Management | Veterans service organizations and mission-driven nonprofits are a strong cultural fit for this population; salary expectations often require adjustment |
| Education & Training | LOW (score 1) | Training & Development, Curriculum Development, Leadership Coaching | Training manager and organizational development roles; JROTC instructor for those remaining in education-adjacent work |

**Top Entry KSA Domains:** Leadership & Management · Strategic Planning · Compliance & Risk Management · Program/Project Management · Communications & Briefing · Training & Development

**Typical Entry Roles:**
- Operations Manager / Director of Operations — broad applicability; senior NCOs (E-8/E-9) and O-4/O-5 officers are competitive
- Program Manager / Project Manager (PMP certification bridges gap) — strong demand in CSRA contractor market; PMP is the most valuable credential investment for this population
- Human Resources Manager / Training & Development Manager — leverages counseling, evaluation, and development experience from NCO/officer duties
- Management Analyst / Program Analyst (GS-0343) — federal pathway; common first-career civilian role before transitioning to private sector
- JROTC Senior Army Instructor — accessible for retiring LTC/CSM; structured transition with continued connection to military environment

**CaliberPath Service Match:**
- *Career Compass Coaching:* The identity transition is often harder than the skills translation for this population. Early sessions should address the mindset shift from rank-based authority to demonstrated-value leadership. Competency assessment should surface leadership behaviors at the Advanced and Expert levels that this population has but struggles to articulate in civilian language. Resume translation from military evaluations (OERs/NCOERs) to civilian achievement language is a high-value early deliverable.
- *Stream 4 Assessment Focus:* Full UPLS Leadership competency suite — Strategic Thinking, Decision Making, Coaching & Development, Change Management, and Organizational Awareness. Many in this population operate at Advanced or Expert level in leadership domains and need coaching toward articulation, not development.

## Adjacency Matrix Source Notes

The sector destinations and scores in each profile are derived directly from the CaliberPath Sector Adjacency Matrix v1.0, computed from 684 KSA files across 35 sectors using normalized cluster_tags (T-CROSSWALK-01, 2026-03-16). Scores represent the count of shared cluster_tag domains between the MOS category's primary competency domains and each civilian sector's KSA library: HIGH = 7+ shared domains, MEDIUM = 4–6, LOW = 2–3, MARGINAL = 1. Adjacency scores are a starting point for coaching conversation, not a deterministic ranking — individual client competency profiles, geographic preferences, salary targets, and credential investment capacity all affect which sector is actually the best fit. Use the adjacency data to frame the option space, then narrow based on the client's full profile.
